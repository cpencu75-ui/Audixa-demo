👂 Curation Guide — How to pick the best result
The 30-second test: Does it hook you in the first 30 seconds? If you skip before that, your listeners will too. The best generation hooks immediately — never rationalize a weak opening.
Listen for the lift: Great AI music has a moment where something unexpected happens — a harmonic surprise, a texture shift, a dynamic peak. If nothing surprises you, regenerate.
The silence test: Play it with your eyes closed in a quiet room. If it sounds like background noise from a video game lobby, it's generic. If you feel something — a mood, a memory, a sense of tension — it is worth keeping.
The loop test (for stock music): Can you loop it for 3 minutes without noticing the repetition? Perfect for YouTube background. If it gets repetitive in 45 seconds, add more arc parameters.
Platform fit test: Play it while watching the actual content it's meant for. YouTube tutorial → does it disappear behind the voice? TikTok → does it demand attention in 3 seconds?

Chapter 05

Daily 90-minute session

The repeatable structure that turns 90 minutes of focused work into 5–10 finished tracks.

The session structure

  1. 1
    Open the dashboard (2 min) — Check your KPIs. Note your track count and revenue estimate. Open Best Performers to remind yourself of what has worked. This anchors the session.
  2. 2
    Generate catalog prompts (10 min) — Catalog Studio, target niche, count 10, Full Random variation. Generate. You now have 10 engineered prompts each with a quality score and commercial hook.
  3. 3
    Curation round 1 — prompts (5 min) — Save all A-score prompts immediately. Review B-score: save if the commercial hook is strong. Discard C-score unless the concept justifies regenerating with adjusted parameters.
  4. 4
    Generate in Suno (40 min) — Paste each saved prompt into Suno's Style field. Generate 2–3 variations. Apply the five curation tests. Keep the best variation from each prompt.
  5. 5
    Feedback loop (3 min) — Return to Audixa. Press 👍 or 👎 on each prompt card based on the Suno result. Fifteen seconds per prompt. Builds your Best Performers library over time.
  6. 6
    Metadata batch (15 min) — Click Generate Metadata for each kept track. Copy title, description, and 15 tags into a spreadsheet. One row per track — ready to paste on upload day.
  7. 7
    Upload day — once per week (15 min) — Batch upload to Pond5 and/or AudioJungle. Paste metadata from your spreadsheet. Set pricing. Submit. Review results the following week and adjust niche focus.
A consistent 90-minute daily session produces 5–10 submitted tracks per day. That is 150–300 tracks per month — enough to reach the $400–1,200 income tier within 60–90 days of consistent operation.

The five curation tests — applied in 90 seconds per track

  • ⏱
    30-second test — Skip to 0:00. Does it hook you immediately? If you want to skip, your buyer will too. Fail = regenerate.
  • 🎵
    Lift test — Is there one moment that surprises you? A dynamic shift, a harmonic move, a new texture? No lift = generic = low sales probability.
  • 🔇
    Silence test — Eyes closed, no distractions. Do you feel anything? A mood, a memory, a tension? Nothing = generic. Something = keep it.
  • 🔁
    Loop test (stock only) — Can you loop it for 3 minutes without the repetition becoming irritating? Stock buyers play tracks on repeat. If it grates at 90 seconds, regenerate with a more developed arc.
  • 📱
    Platform fit test — Play it alongside the actual content it is meant for. YouTube: does it disappear behind the voice? TikTok: does it demand attention in 3 seconds?
Chapter 06

All six studios

What each studio does, when to use it, and the one thing most producers miss.

Prompt Studio

What it does: Generates 1–5 Suno prompts from your description or parameters, with music theory and production vocabulary applied automatically.

Three levels: Easy Mode (vibe + energy + generate — three clicks), Creator Mode (genre, instrument, key, BPM), Pro Mode (all 30+ parameters).

What most producers miss: The Describe field. Write "cinematic tension build for a heist scene, no drums" and press Quick Generate. The engine reads your concept and fills parameters before generating. Faster than selecting manually for clear ideas.

The Lucky Fusion button selects a random genre fusion and generates a full engineered prompt. Some of your best-selling tracks will come from fusions you would never have chosen deliberately.

Lyric Architect

What it does: Generates Suno-formatted lyrics with structural tags ([Verse 1], [Chorus], [Bridge], etc.) plus a separate Style Prompt for Suno's Style field.

Critical: Paste lyrics into Suno's Lyrics field and the Style Prompt into Suno's Style field. Never mix them. Suno uses the two fields independently.

What most producers miss: The Tone / Voice setting. "Third person, cinematic" produces a completely different vocal delivery from "First person, raw and intimate" — even with identical instrumental parameters. This is how you differentiate tracks within the same niche.

Extension Engine

What it does: Generates continuation prompts for Suno's Extend feature — preserving key, mode, and feel while adding structural development.

Workflow: Paste your original prompt → choose type (Continue, Build, Climax, Cool Down) → generate → paste into Suno Extend.

What most producers miss: Chaining extensions. Build → Climax → Cool Down produces a complete three-act arc across three Suno Extend operations. Result: a 6–8 minute track with a full musical journey — premium licensing value on every stock platform.

Long Mix Studio

What it does: Plans and generates prompts for extended sessions (20 minutes to 8 hours), with harmonically compatible transitions between segments.

Best use case: YouTube sleep, focus, and ambient channels. A 3-hour "Deep Focus Music" video earns AdSense and accumulates watch time that feeds the algorithm — compounding reach over time.

Catalog Studio

What it does: Generates 5–50 commercial prompts for a specific niche in one operation, each optimized for platform and buyer behavior.

FAQ

Real questions from real catalog producers. Direct answers.
Can I legally sell AI-generated music on Pond5?
Yes. Pond5 and AudioJungle accept AI-assisted music. You must disclose it is AI-generated during submission — both platforms now include a checkbox for this. Suno's Commercial plan grants full commercial rights to distribute and sell generated tracks. Always verify current platform policies as they continue to evolve.
My tracks keep getting rejected. What am I doing wrong?
The most common reasons: (1) Audio quality — normalize to -14 LUFS and export as WAV, not MP3. (2) Arrangement too repetitive — use the Extension Engine to add structural development. (3) Generic AI sound — always pick from 3 Suno variations, never the first result. (4) Incomplete metadata — all Pond5 fields are required. The Metadata Generator fills every field correctly.
Should I use the same prompt on multiple AI music platforms?
Yes — this is a recommended strategy. The same Audixa prompt produces different results on Suno, Udio, and Mureka. Run one prompt across multiple platforms and compare outputs. You increase the chance of finding an exceptional result, and your catalog gains sonic diversity that both reviewers and buyers respond to.
Which AI model produces the best prompts — Claude, GPT-4, or Gemini?
All three produce excellent results using Audixa's system prompts. Claude has the deepest music theory comprehension — best for orchestral and jazz genres. GPT-4o excels at lyric writing and creative variation. Gemini is free, fast, and excellent for high-volume catalog production. Start with Gemini, add Claude for premium niches.
How many tracks before I see consistent monthly income?
Thirty approved tracks in a single focused niche is the reliable minimum. Below 30, sales are sporadic. Between 30 and 100 tracks, monthly income becomes consistent and predictable. Above 100 tracks in 2–3 niches, the catalog compounds — older tracks continue to sell as newer tracks drive search visibility.
What does the revenue estimate in the dashboard assume?
It uses a conservative model: average license price of $12 at a 5% monthly sell-through rate, which equals 1 sale per 20 tracks per month. In practice, your best tracks sell more frequently and your weakest may not sell at all. The estimate is calibrated to reflect realistic early-stage income, not ceiling potential.
My Best Performers list is empty. How do I build it?
After using a prompt in Suno and evaluating the result, return to Audixa and click 👍 Worked great on that prompt card. One click per session. After 5–10 sessions you will have a meaningful collection of proven formulas. This is the single most important habit to build in your first two weeks of production.
The app does not remember my settings between sessions. Is this normal?
Yes. Parameter selections (dropdowns, sliders) reset on each browser open — by design to prevent stale configurations. However, Session Recipes, Saved Prompts, Best Performers, Artist DNA, and prompt history all persist in your browser's localStorage. Use Session Recipes to restore your best configurations in one click at the start of each session.
Can I use Audixa for music I release on Spotify and streaming platforms?
Absolutely. The prompts are platform-agnostic — use them with any AI music generator and distribute via DistroKid, TuneCore, or direct streaming submission. The Lyric Architect is particularly useful for structured songs with chorus hooks rather than instrumental background tracks. Legal Clarity

What you own — and how to own more

The honest guide to AI music copyright in 2026. Not legal advice — but facts you need before you publish.

The core reality

Suno Pro and Premier plans grant you commercial use rights — you can sell your music on Pond5, AudioJungle, Spotify, and Gumroad. But "commercial use rights" is not the same as copyright ownership.

The US Copyright Office generally does not register purely AI-generated music as copyrightable — because copyright requires human creativity. This matters for Content ID, ASCAP/BMI registration, and sync licensing, which all require clear copyright ownership.

The UMG v. Suno lawsuit (fair use ruling expected summer 2026) and Sony's ongoing litigation could significantly change this landscape. Monitor developments before making long-term business decisions based on AI music rights.
